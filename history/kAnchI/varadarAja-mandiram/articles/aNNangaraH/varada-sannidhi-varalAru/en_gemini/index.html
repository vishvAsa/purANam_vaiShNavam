Written by Śrī Kāñcī Prativādibhayankaram Aṇṇangarāchāriyar, who holds the hereditary right of sacred water (tīrtham maryādai) and teaching (adhyāpaka mirāsu) in Dēvapperumāḷ Sannidhi.

Maṅgaḷācharaṇam (Invocation)

“Divyāvayava saundarya divyābharaṇa hētavē, Dantāvaḷagirīśāya dēvarājāya maṅgaḷam.” (by Maṇavāḷamāmunikaḷ.)

Chapter One

  1. The Lord, hailed by the world as Ubaya Vibhūtinātha (Lord of both worlds), has revealed His immense grace in this mundane realm, Līlā Vibhūti, more than in Śrī Vaikuṇṭham, the Nitya Vibhūti. Our Āḻvār [^3_1] has conveyed this truth through the pashuram “Kaḍal ñālattu aḷi mikkan” (Tiruvāymoḻi 4-8-5). The reason for the Lord’s greater affection for this dark world, compared to Tripād Vibhūti where Nitya Sūris and Muktas constantly enjoy His full service, is primarily to alleviate the suffering of Samsāris. Also, the abundance of Divya Desams scattered across this earthly realm is a major factor. Among the hundreds of Divya Desams that flourish here, three are particularly revered as “Kōyil, Tirumalai, Perumāḷ Kōyil.” These are traditionally known as Śrīrangam, Tiruvēṅkaṭamalai, and Śrī Kāñcī.

  2. One might ask why these three Divya Desams hold special pre-eminence among all.

[[P4]] 2 ŚRĪRĀMĀNUJAṉ

Eminent scholars state that it is because these three Divya Desams alone have been celebrated in the Tiruviruttam. It is noteworthy that Śrīrangam is glorified as “Porunīrttiruvarangā aruḷāy” (28), Tiruvēṅkaṭamalai as “Perumāṉmalaiyō Tiruvēṅkaṭam eṉṟu kaṟkiṉṟa vāsakamē” (60), and Śrī Kāñcī as “Viṇṇōr toḻuṅkaṇṇaṉ Veḵkāūtu” (25). Since other Divya Desams are not praised in the initial Tiruviruttam, Śrī Vaiṣṇava scholars uphold the notion that the Āḻvār’s intention was to give primacy to these three Divya Desams.

  1. Among the aforementioned three holy places, Kāñcīpuram enjoys paramount importance. The reason for this is the Śrī Sūkti of Āḻvār himself. Our Dēvapperumāḷ is glorified by Nammāḻvār in the very first pashuram of the initial Tiruviruttam and also in the first pashuram of the final Tiruvāymoḻi. In the first pashuram of Tiruviruttam, he said “Imayōr talaivā! Mey niṉṟu kēṭṭaruḷāy” (Oh, Lord of the celestials! Please listen with truth). The reference to “Imayōr talaivā!” directly refers to the Lord Dēvārāja. Similarly, in the first pashuram of Tiruvāymoḻi, “Amararkaḷ adhipati” also refers to Dēvapperumāḷ. His name “Praṇatārtiharana” (remover of suffering for those who surrender) is invoked when Nammāḻvār says “Tūyaraṟu chuṭaraḍiyeṉṟu maruḷichcheytu” (meaning, He bestowed the radiant feet that remove distress). The concept of “Prathama shatake vīkṣya vādam” established by the Vēdānta teachers also confirms this interpretation.

  2. The pre-eminence of this holy place can also be understood from the fact that it is commonly referred to as “Perumāḷ Kōyil” (Lord’s Temple). While all Divya Desams in the world are ultimately the Lord’s temples, it is significant that this particular one has been known as “Perumāḷ Kōyil” since ancient times. Many commentaries have emerged for the Brahmasūtras, known as Śārīrakamīmāṁsā. Other commentaries are referred to by their authors’ names, such as Śaṅkarabhāṣyam, Madhvabhāṣyam, and Nīlakaṇḍabhāṣyam. However, the commentary composed by Svāmi Rāmānuja is uniquely known throughout the world as Śrībhāṣyam, not “Rāmānujabhāṣyam”. Similarly, this place alone is referred to as “Perumāḷ Kōyil.”

  3. Listen further to the special characteristics of this holy place. When people from outside come here, they say they are going to “Perumāḷ Kōyil”. When they arrive and proceed from the entrance to the Sannidhi, they say they are going to “Kōyil”. After entering the temple, when they proceed to worship Śrī Hastigiri Shikharojjvala Pārijāta, they say they are going to “Tirumalai”. Thus, it is appropriate to understand that the three Divya Desams – Kōyil, Tirumalai, Perumāḷ Kōyil – are all present here.

  3. On the fourth day morning, it is the Śēṣa Vāhanam (Serpent Vehicle) with the Lord adorned as Paramapadhanātha. This matches the recitation of Nāṉmugan Tiruvandādi, with verses like “āṅkāravāramadu kēṭṭu aḻalumīḻum pūṅkāravaravaṇaiyāṉ” (the beautiful Kāravam serpent-bed that emits fire upon hearing the roar of pride) and “viritturaittaveṇ nāgattunnai” (You on the broad white serpent). The aforementioned Tātāchārya used this tradition as a strong basis and interpreted the line “Thennaṅgar selvam muṭṭum tiruttivaittāṉ vāḻiyē” (Long live he who fully reformed the wealth of the Southern Lord) in Uḍaiyavar’s Vāḻi Tirunāmam as Śrī Rāmānuja orchestrating this ‘ploy’ to convert a Vadakalai Dēvasthānam into a Thenkalai one. His lines are: “nīṣaṅkau ca karadavān vyājāharat: svapnēṣu na vāsabhā; yyāṃśca kṣaguṣaḥ saṁsthitihetih; upadiśya nijalōchanē ḷōjā nāne tarvikyatāṁ vasurahetu:”… [^42_1] Those who wish to know more about this in detail can find it in our Satsampradāya Bhāskara book, published in Sanskrit and South Indian languages recently.

Another official took a list of 18 Thenkalai tirumaṇkaḷ. The records confirm that only Thenkalai tirumaṇkāppus were prevalent in this Dēvasthānam in ancient times, just as in other Divya Desams. Chapter Fifteen – About Āttāṉ Jīyar

  1. The advocate has written that Śrīśailēśadayāpātram Taniyan (eulogy) was allowed to be recited in this Dēvasthānam as a reward for Āttāṉ Jīyar’s special service to Dēvapperumāḷ. He attempts to appear pleased by presenting it as if Rāmānuja Pātram was mentioned here before, and Śrīśailēśa Pātram was introduced later for a specific reason. The Pātram Taniyan appeared only during Maṇavāḷamāmunikaḷ’s time, specifically about him. By the Lord’s command, it spread everywhere simultaneously, ensuring eternal prosperity and auspiciousness without any defect. In the places where attempts were made to introduce Rāmānuja Pātram (due to disputes), injunctions and royal punishments were imposed. We have published a detailed book in all languages, Rāmānuja Dayāpātram Pathya Charitram (History of the Ramanuja Dayapatram Verses), clarifying many such truths. It is known worldwide. Therefore, authentic scholars already understand the truth, and so there is no need for us to write about it in detail here.

  2. Was Āttāṉ Jīyar’s special service rendered only at Dēvapperumāḷ’s Dēvasthānam, or also at Tūppul, Perumbūdūr, Tiruppuṭkuḻi, Tiruvuḷḷūr, or other places? No. If Śrīśailēśadayāpātram was permitted everywhere for Dēvapperumāḷ’s Sannidhi service, how great is that? Authentic scholars should examine this. In the judgment of Śrī Kṛṣṇasāmi Iyer, the long-standing lawyer for the Vadakalai community and later a High Court judge, regarding the Perumbūdūr case, it is inscribed, “The Tātāchāryas lost their honors and respect because of their animosity towards Śrīśailēśadayāpātram Taniyan (eulogy) from their ancestral tradition”. This should be recited daily at all three times.

  1. About two hundred years ago, a Parakāla Svāmi from Mysore composed the Rāmānuja Dayāpātram Taniyan and began reciting it in Tirunārāyaṇapuram. With a desire to introduce it in the South and in some Divya Desams, he went on a pilgrimage to the South. He came to Kāñcīpuram, but the Tātāchāryas refused. He left after uttering a curse. It is a universally known history. Later, other Vadakalai individuals, encouraged by that Parakāla Svāmi, started reciting Rāmānuja Pātram some years later, but it was stopped as described above. It is clear from temple records that disputes started then. It is also clear that disputes started simultaneously in several temples. The Rāmānuja Pātram Taniyan appeared only two or three years before these disputes began. From its inception, the Pādukā Sahasram has been widely revered.
