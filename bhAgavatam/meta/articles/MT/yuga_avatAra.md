The bhAgavata, as a later text, certainly shows influences of the ancient tradition from the early pa~ncharAtra expounded in the mahAbhArata. For example, the veda-restoring haMsa comes in both in the kR^ita. A complete treatment of the vibhava-s of the bhAgavata will take a lot of effort; hence, I'll just restrict myself to some snippets. The treta is said to feature the following vibhava-s:

> viShNur yaj~naH pR^ishni-garbhaH  
> sarvadeva urukramaH |
vR^iShAkapir jayantash cha  
> urugAya itIryate ||

## yajnaH, urugAya, urukrama
This list is quite striking, for the bhAgavata-kAra is clearly drawing on rather ancient names and motifs that are mostly from the shruti: e.g., the first two epithets are a paraphrase of the famous vedic statement: "yaj~no vai viShNuH |". Similarly, urukrama and urugAya are vedic synonyms of viShNu with deep early Indo-European roots that faded away in later tradition.  

## vRShAkapi
The enigmatic figure vR^iShAkapi (literally a virile ape) appears in a single eponymous sUkta in the RV (10.86) and AV-vulgate (20.126). He is a close friend of indra, like viShNu, but the sUkta never explicitly identifies him viShNu.  
However, when he reappears in the mahAbharata in the same section that propounds early pa~ncharAtra, he is unambiguously identified with viShNu. Moreover, there is a tendency to identify him with varAha:

> kapir varAhaH shreShThash cha  
> dharmash cha vR^iSha uchyate |
tasmAd vR^iShAkapiM prAha  
kashyapo mAM prajApatiH || (Mbh "Pune" 12.330.024)  

> kapi is the foremost of boars, and dharma is said to be vR^iSha (c.f., shiva's bull is said to be dharma). Hence, kashyapa prajApati called me vR^iShAkapi.

This raises the possibility that perhaps even in the shruti, vR^iShAkapi, the naughty friend of indra, should be understood as viShNu.

## pRshnigarbha
Then there is the name pR^ishnigarbha. It occurs first in the same early pa~NcharAtra section of the mahAbhArata multiple times and is explained thus:

> pR^ishnir ity uchyate chAnnaM  
> vedA Apo .amR^itaM tathA |  
mamaitAni sadA garbhe  
> pR^ishnigarbhas tato hy aham ||  
> 
> R^iShayaH prAhur evaM mAM  
trita-kUpAbhi-pAtitam   |  
pR^ishnigarbha tritaM pAhIty   
ekata-dvita-pAtitam ||
tataH sa brahmaNaH putra  
> Adyo R^iShivaras tritaH |  
uttatArodapAnAd vai  
pR^ishnigarbhAnukIrtanAt || 12.328.40-42

> Food, the veda-s, water and ambrosia are said to be pR^ishni. These are always in my womb; therefore, I am indeed  pR^ishnigarbha.  
The R^iShi-s have said that trita, who had fallen in the well, [invoked] me thus:  
> "O pR^ishnigarbha, protect trita, who has been cast [into the well] by ekata and dvita."  
Then that Trita, the son of brahman, the foremost of the R^iShi-s, ascended from the well, indeed, by reciting [the praise of] pR^ishnigarbha.

The word pR^ishni-garbha is etymologized in the sense of the contents of viShNu's womb. Further, interestingly, this etymology is connected with a reflex of the tale of trita of ultimately vedic provenance. 

However, in the shruti, the word pR^ishni-garbha (in plural) refers to those born of the womb of pR^ishni, which would only imply the marut-s (e.g., RV 10.123.1). In the shrauta ritual, there is a specific incantation were viShNu is invoked along with the marut-s (the evayAmarut sUkta, RV 5.87). Hence, we posit that viShNu among the marut-s was probably known as pR^ishnigarbha; however, this obscure meaning of vedic provenance was forgotten and the name re-etymologized as above in the early pa~ncharAtra tradition.+++(4)+++

## jayanta
Finally, there is the epithet jayanta. This name was traditionally applied to the son of indra; hence, it appears that here, interestingly, viShNu is identified with the son of indra.

## Conclusion
In conclusion, despite being a late text, the bhAgavata draws from some rather ancient motifs that filtered down through early pa~ncharAtra and were incorporated into its system of vibhava-s.
